The overall picture seems to be that as green energy tech gets cheaper, it'll gain widespread usage just for cost reasons. Sea level will rise, species will die, and weather will get more extreme, but it doesn't seem like there'll be some major disaster that wipes out mankind or nature. Anyways, enough intro. Fusion

  If fusion works, it'd be really ideal. The fuel is most likely really cheap: deuterium from seawater, tritium "from a lithium blanket using neutrons produced in the fusion reaction itself". (11) It also has no bad byproducts. The main cost would be building and maintaining them. We have working fusion right now, problem is it uses up more power than it puts in.

  The europeans have the biggest project going on right now, ITER. Its predecessor, JET, had a .67 power-out-to-power-in ratio. (10) ITER's goal is to get that ratio to 5, producing 5x as much power as the amount you put in. (11) The main problem is it's huge and complicated, so construction is expected to finish by 2025. (11) Full experiments aren't expected until 2035. (11) After ITER's done, the next step up is a full demonstration fusion power generator, which they plan on calling DEMO. It isn't expected to be finished until 2048. It would output 2000 MW, meaning that we'd need about 1300 of them to power the whole world.

  Another fusion project is being done by Commonwealth Fusion. They're using a really strong magnet to do fusion, which will hopefully mean that the machine itself doesn't have to be as big and complicated. (13) Their reactor, SPARC, is planned to output 100 MW of power with 1/65 the volume of ITER. (13) SPARC's sequel, ARC, is expecrted to output 500 MW of power and be half the diameter of ITER. (14) According to their website, the big magnet is in progress, SPARC will begin to be made in 2021, and ARC will begin to be made in 2025. (15) From this we can probably assume that the big magnet is supposed to be finished by 2021 and SPARC is supposed to be finished by 2025. Note that that's the same year that ITER is supposed to be finished.

Batteries

  Renewables tend to be inconsistent. The main way to fix this is getting lots of batteries. Right now batteries cost about $156/KWh, but that's expected to decrease to $100/KWh by 2023. (16) This is down from $1100/KWh in 2010. (16) Assuming the world needs half a day's electric worth of batteries, the battery cost right now would be $4.7 trillion, 5% of the GWP.

  Assuming costs exponentially decrease and the rate of decrease stays the same as it was between 2010 and now (i.e. 87% per 9 years): The battery cost for the world would decrease to $611 billion by 2028, .71% of the GWP. It would decrease to $79 billion by 2037, .092% of the GWP. Note that this also assumes that power usage and GWP don't increase, which are both probably both very wrong, but they push the % of GWP in opposite directions so my numbers are probably accurate-ish.

  Those numbers are cool and all but "generic average battery" isn't a product that's on the market and can be used. An example of an actual battery that can be bought right now is the Tesla Powerwall 2. It costs $6500 and stores 13.5 KWh. (17) We'd need 2.2 billion of them to store half the world's electric use, which would cost $14 trillion, 17% of the GWP.

  Another alternative to batteries is pulling our nighttime power from somewhere where it's daytime, i.e. making big ocean power cables, but I haven't bothered to look into this.

Removing Carbon

  There's a lot of possible methods for removing carbon from the atmosphere, so I'll just let this article list them out for me. (42) Most of them seem to cost about $100/ton of CO2. Given current world emissions, this would mean we'd need to spend $3.6 trillion a year to store all the world's emissions, or 2.8% of the GWP. Definitely not ideal, but doable if necessary. Of course, some of these solutions might not as scalable as we need. You can only plant so many trees, for example. Many of them show a lot of promise, though: the ones that end up with raw CO2 as a product can just put that CO2 into containers and put those containers somewhere safe, scaling nearly infinitely.

Emissions by Industry

  (27) lists the sources of emissions by sector. One important thing to note is that electricity and heat only make up 25% of the total. This means that if we put our entire power grid on green energy, we'd only drop our emissions by 25%. Other industries need to start using electric rather than gas motors before we can see an improvement there.

Weather

  Source (37) talks about weather changes that have been happening and will probably continue. Summary: increased temperature changes, more rain, more severe storms.

Conclusion

  According to the sources I found, it seems that we'll follow the UN model's RCP 4.5 pathway, peaking emissions around 2040 before switching over to green energy because of green power, batteries, and electric motors becoming cheaper. Nuclear fusion is also possible, but that's still doesn't work yet. Carbon capture also might work too: it'd use take around 2.8% of the GWP to capture all of our yearly emissions.

  All together, if we end up following RCP 4.5 as I'm guessing, we'll end up with a 0.26-meter sea level rise by 2065 and 0.47-meter rise by 2100. Global warming will probably result in increased temperature changes, more rain, and more severe storms. It'll probably also result in the extinction of some species.

  There are ways to make the outcome better, like making policy changes (which I didn't really look into) and lowering our own carbon footprints (see #carbon footprints above).
